Artifical Intelligence (AI)

November 24, 2020
Deep Learning

AI (Artificial Intelligence) is simply giving computers the ability to emulate the cognitive process of a human. In other words, computers can learn and solve a given problem as a human does. There are two types of AI: strong AI and weak AI. The strong AI has the level of intelligence equivalent to a human being and can make decisions by itself, which means it acts like it has an identity. On the other hand, weak AI does not have its own ego but can carry out the partial capacity of a human. It is expertise only in a certain field such as education, business, medicine and etc. Weak AI is already deeply established in our society and even has exceeded human performance in some areas, including board games (go, chess), detection of breast cancer, face recognition, and voice recognition. 

The AI went from being based on illustrating the definition from a human's perspective to allowing the computers to narrow down the answer by itself based on the data collected. The primitive model of AI was a knowledge-based system. For example, the computer learned the set definition of a dog as "an animal with 4 legs, 2 eyes, 1 nose, and a tail". However, the method was limited and erroneous because there were so many other things that can be defined as the aforementioned explanation. To resolve the conundrum, computer scientists suggested a new technique called machine learning that is focused on data, where computers educate themselves without being explicitly programmed. Now, computers are able to determine common patterns or underlying meanings based on the data collected and predict the solution for a new piece of data inputted.

Within the category of machine learning, there are supervised learning and unsupervised learning. Supervised learning provides the computer both inputs and outputs of a problem and guides it to discover the program aiming for the target value (the answer). For instance, classification, a type of supervised learning, allows computers to classify the results according to previously collected data. The target value does not contain interrelationships so the values are categorized into a few groups. Another example is regression. This system analyzes a set of statistics by examining the relationship between a dependent and an independent variable, which means that the target value has a correlation. In contrast, unsupervised learning only provides inputs and computers to make their own pattern or standard to cluster the data.
